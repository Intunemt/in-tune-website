Music Therapy is a powerful, evidenced based clinical field with lots to offer clients.  However, it is often misunderstood or folks think they know everything there is about it.  Heck, I’m 15 years into the profession and I’m still learning about music therapy’s identity!  Here are 5 misconceptions about music therapy.  

1. It’s a child’s therapy 

Myth!  Music Therapists work with people as young as those in the NICU and as old as those peacefully passing in hospice- and everyone in between!  True, music therapy is a prevalent treatment for kids with autism or developmental disability, but adults absolutely benefit from services.  Throughout my career I have worked with adults in a mental health focused model of music therapy, focusing on goals that benefit a complex, emotional, stressed but-still-have-to- get-the-kids-to-their-next-thing members of society.

2. You have to be musical or have musical talent to benefit

Myth!  As a Board Certified Music Therapist, I have extensive training in how to bring the power of music to non-musicians.  It would be an absolute shame if only those with musical talent could engage in music therapy.  I’m glad there is nothing further from the truth.  So what does it mean?  This might look like me knowing chord structures, qualities of tonality, musical form, music elements such as tempo, rhythm, timbre, pitch that are best called for in a situation while to the non musician it seems like “Yeah! That totally sounds like how I’m feeling!”

3. It’s just about listening to music

Myth! Don’t get me wrong, there is a whole section of music therapy techniques that we call “receptive” that are like listening to music but if you really want to experience the power of music, you have to get involved!  Its not uncommon for music therapists to incorporate recorded music, live music, instrument playing, songwriting and more tailored to your needs into a session.  Somebody grab the egg shakers!

4. The main goal of music therapy is to calm or soothe

Myth!  Calming and soothing can be part of music therapy work, but some of the most powerful moments I’ve seen in music therapy are when clients get out of their comfort zone and challenge themselves within the safety/container of the music.  I’ve witnessed painful realizations, expressions of repressed feelings from trauma, horrible moments validated by music, grief, shame and other difficult emotions come to the surface.  But with music, it provides an avenue for experience, expression, acceptance, change beyond words. This can be especially helpful when emotional pain cannot be encompassed by words, it needs more.

5. Music therapists are the only ones who should be using music to do therapy

Myth!  Music therapists do not own music!  I’ve seen many professionals (social workers, psychologists, physical therapists) use music to benefit the work they are doing.  However, if someone is calling their services music therapy, by music therapy’s definition, they should be a board certified music therapist. Music therapists’ training hones in on what music interventions would be the most effective, how to adapt them in the moment and how to ensure the music isn’t doing harm.
